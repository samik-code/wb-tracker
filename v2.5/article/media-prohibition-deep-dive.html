West Bengal Government Issues Circular Restricting Officials' Media Interactions: A Factual Deep Dive

Published: May 21, 2026 | Last Updated: May 22, 2026 • Factual Analysis • Source-Based & Neutral

Introduction: What Happened?

On May 19, 2026 — ten days after the new BJP-led government under Chief Minister Suvendu Adhikari took oath — the West Bengal Chief Secretary’s office issued Circular No. 139-CS/2026.

The circular is titled:
“Dissemination of Information by any member of the All India Service, West Bengal Civil Service, West Bengal Police Service and other employees working under the Government of West Bengal.”

In simple terms, it reminds all state government officials that they cannot speak to the media, share official information publicly, or criticise government policies without prior permission.

👉 Official PDF: Circular No. 139-CS/2026

This article explains exactly what the circular says, why it was issued, the legal background, reactions from different sides, how it compares to similar rules in other states, and what it means for transparency and public information in West Bengal.

Part 1: What Does the Circular Actually Say?

Who Does It Cover?

The circular applies to:

  • Members of All India Services (IAS, IPS, IFS, etc.)
  • West Bengal Civil Service (WBCS)
  • West Bengal Police Service (WBPS)
  • Other government employees, including those in police, jails, autonomous bodies, boards, corporations, and state-funded educational institutions.

It covers a wide range of state employees, not just senior officers.

Main Prohibitions (Key Points from the Circular)

The circular uses strong language such as “complete prohibition” and requires prior government sanction in many cases. According to multiple news reports quoting the document directly:

  1. No sharing of information with media: “Complete prohibition, without any governmental order, on any direct or indirect communication of any document or information with the press by the members of the services.”
  2. No participation in media programmes: “Complete prohibition, except with prior sanction, on the participation or association of any member of the services in any sponsored or privately produced media programme…”
  3. No adverse criticism: Officials cannot “indulge in any adverse criticism of any policy or decision of the Central Government or the State Government.”
  4. No contribution to media: Strict limits on writing articles, participating in broadcasts, or any activity that could strain relations between the State and Centre or other states.

Important note: The circular repeatedly states that these rules already exist under long-standing service conduct rules. It is essentially a fresh reminder to ensure strict compliance.

Part 2: Legal Basis and Constitutional Context

The circular cites three existing sets of rules that have been in force for decades:

  • All India Services (Conduct) Rules, 1968 — Rule 7 (prohibits adverse criticism of government policy).
Official link to AIS Conduct Rules
  • West Bengal Service (Duties, Rights and Obligations of the Government Employees) Rules, 1980
  • West Bengal Government Servants’ Conduct Rules, 1959

Constitutional angle:
Article 19(1)(a) of the Indian Constitution guarantees freedom of speech and expression to all citizens, including government servants. However, Article 19(2) allows reasonable restrictions in the interest of public order, sovereignty, security, etc. Courts have generally upheld conduct rules for government employees as valid restrictions to maintain administrative neutrality and prevent unauthorised leaks.

The circular does not create new law — it reiterates existing rules. Legal experts note that while the government has the right to enforce discipline, any restrictions must be proportionate and not overly vague.

Part 3: Political Reactions

Trinamool Congress (TMC) strongly criticised the move.
Party general secretary Abhishek Banerjee shared the circular on social media and called the phrase “complete prohibition” a warning to enforce silence among government employees. He described it as an attempt to “strangle democracy.”

Sources:
  • The Hindu
  • India Today

The BJP government has framed the move as a step to maintain discipline, prevent leaks of sensitive information, and ensure a single coherent official position on government matters.

Part 4: The 48-Hour Revision: Government Alteration (May 21, 2026)

Following substantial public backlash and intense criticism from opposition parties, the West Bengal government made its first significant administrative adjustment on May 21, 2026 — exactly 48 hours after the original circular was reported.

A revised administrative memorandum, signed by Additional Chief Secretary Rajesh Pandey, was issued to narrow the scope of the sweeping gag order. Under the new directive, the "complete prohibition" is restricted "ONLY" to regular establishments and parastatals under the direct administrative control of the state government.

👉 Source: Telegraph India (May 22, 2026)

While the state government described the alteration as a logical administrative refinement to prevent overreach, opposition parties and public sector unions viewed the scramble as a strategic retreat. Union leaders, including teachers' associations, noted that the inclusion of "parastatal organisations" could still be interpreted broadly to restrict autonomous educational bodies and state-aided institutions.

Part 5: Is This Unique to West Bengal?

No. Similar instructions have been issued by governments of different political parties in several states over the years.

Examples:

  • Uttar Pradesh (2024): The government issued guidelines barring state employees from talking, posting, or writing about government decisions on any media platform (social, print, electronic) without prior permission.
Source: The Print (2024)
  • Bihar: The state government has issued guidelines restricting government employees from posting political or religious views, expressing personal opinions on government policies, judicial decisions, or interacting with media on social media without prior approval. Violations can lead to disciplinary action.
Sources: NDTV (April 2026) | The Hindu (January 2026)
  • Jammu & Kashmir (2017 and later): The government barred employees from criticising government policies or engaging in political commentary on social media. The order was originally issued in 2017 and reiterated in subsequent years.
Sources: Al Jazeera (December 2017) | Official 2023 Circular (J&K GAD)
  • Maharashtra (2025): In July 2025, the government tightened social media usage rules for employees, prohibiting them from publicly criticising the policies of the Maharashtra Government or the Government of India.
Sources: Times of India (July 29, 2025) | NDTV (July 28, 2025)

What stands out in West Bengal’s case is the timing (just days after a new government took charge) and the repeated use of the phrase “complete prohibition.” The underlying rules, however, are standard across many states and at the central level.

Part 6: What Does This Mean for Ordinary Citizens?

When government employees are restricted from speaking, citizens often rely on the Right to Information (RTI) Act, 2005.

Limitations of RTI:

  • Section 8 lists many exemptions (national security, commercial secrets, personal information, etc.).
  • The Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Act, 2023 amended RTI Section 8(1)(j), removing the earlier “larger public interest” proviso in some cases.
Analysis: AmLegals

Practical challenges with RTI remain: long delays, backlogs, and limited proactive disclosure.

Whistleblower protection is another weak area. The Whistle Blowers Protection Act, 2014 has still not been fully notified and operationalised.

Part 7: Balanced Assessment – What the Rules Get Right and What Raises Concerns

What the government gets right:

  • Preventing leaks of sensitive or confidential information.
  • Avoiding contradictory public statements.
  • Maintaining political neutrality of civil servants.

Genuine concerns raised by critics:

  • The broad language could discourage routine, factual communication with journalists.
  • A “chilling effect” may develop where officials stay completely silent.
  • The timing can be seen as a strong signal of control.

The real test will be how the circular is implemented in practice.

Conclusion

The West Bengal circular of May 19, 2026 is a strong reiteration of conduct rules that have existed for decades and are followed (with varying strictness) by governments across India.

In a democracy, there is a legitimate need for some discipline and confidentiality in the civil service. At the same time, citizens have a right to information about how their tax money is spent. The balance between these two goals depends on careful implementation, strong RTI enforcement, and ideally a fully operational whistleblower protection law.

Citizens, journalists, courts, and civil society will watch closely how this circular is applied. Readers are encouraged to read the primary document (the PDF linked above) and form their own views.
